Integrity in the U.S. Census

Errors and their effects in the 2020 U.S. Census
Volume I, Issue I
Published on January 23, 2023

A couple years after the 2020 Census, it became clear to the U.S. Census Bureau and to journalists that there were serious errors in its final data. Fourteen states had statistically significant miscounts of up to 7%. Three seats in the House of Representatives were assigned to states that should not have them, and consequentially three seats in the House were not assigned to states that deserve them. Is this a normal or expected degree of error? What factors negatively affected the integrity of the 2020 U.S. Census?

Errors in the 2020 U.S. Census

Fourteen States Miscounted in 2020 Census

In May 2022, the United States Census Bureau announced that after completing its Post-Enumeration Survey (PES), it had reason to believe that the 2020 Census significantly overcounted the United States population in eight states and significantly undercounted the United States population in six states.

The eight overcounted states were Delaware, Hawaii, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New York, Ohio, Rhode Island, and Utah. Together, these states were overcounted by 2.9%. That is, for every 100 residents of one of those states, the Census counted an additional 2.9 ghostly "residents" that did not exist.

The six undercounted states were Arkansas, Florida, Illinois, Mississippi, Tennessee, and Texas. These states were undercounted by an aggregated 2.9%. That is, for every 100 residents of one of those states, the Census failed to count 2.9 actual residents.

The 2.9% aggregate overcount and 2.9% aggregate undercount does not imply that the total error in the 2020 Census was zero, however. Because the undercounted states are, on average, significantly more populous than the overcounted states, the 2.9% aggregate undercount carries more "weight". Worked out, the 2020 U.S. Census undercounted the United States population by 0.6%. That is, the U.S. Census Bureau failed to count 6 residents for every 1000 residents of the United States.

Curious readers may investigate themselves and confirm these metrics using this article from the Census Bureau as well as these data from the 2020 U.S. Census for reference.

Effects in American Politics

The errors in the 2020 United States Census do not serve merely as an inconvenience; they significantly affected the reassignment of Congressional seats to each state. According to an analysis conducted by the Heritage Foundation, Florida should have received two additional seats in the House of Representatives. Texas should have received one additional seat. Minnesota and Rhode Island both maintained a seat that they each should have lost. Colorado gained a seat to which the state had no claim. This totals to the erroneous assignment of three Congressional seats, which significantly affects the balance of power in the lesser house of our Congress.

As an aside, let us focus on Minnesota specifically. Alarmingly, Minnesota's population was overcounted by more than 210,000 residents. However, if the U.S. Census Bureau had counted merely 26 fewer residents, the state would have lost its undeserved Congressional seat. Because such a small error can have such a large effect, both collection and processing methodologies must be scrutinized to the greatest extent to mitigate such effects in future Census Bureau surveys.

Errors in the 2010 U.S. Census

An honest discussion about the 2020 U.S. Census necessitates a discussion of the 2010 U.S. Census. If the 2010 Census were erroneous to the same extent as the 2020 Census, the errors are more easily attributed to systematic error and methodology. Here, we consider the results of the 2010 U.S. Census and why Americans should be concerned about the results and methodology of the 2020 U.S. Census.

At First Glance: Near-Perfect Results

At a first glance, it appears that the United States 2010 Census was flawless. The Bureau determined in 2012 that the 2010 Census had undercounted the United States population by 0.01% (~36,000 residents). This is one resident missed for every ten thousand. This appears extraordinarily accurate compared to the 0.6% undercount in the 2020 Census (which is sixty times larger than the error in 2010).

Sixteen Million Residents Missing

Unfortunately, a closer look at the 2010 Census reveals significant errors in its final data. A report published by the non-profit organization Population Reference Bureau (PRB) claims that 16 million residents were missed by the 2010 Census.

These missing residents were offset by approximately 10 million erroneous counts and 6 million imputations: residents added to the census count who were never counted directly but are assumed to exist. In total, the erroneous counts and imputations nearly perfectly offset the significant undercount of the 2010 Census.

State Populations Were Undercounted Equally

Another revealing fact noted by the PRB report linked above is that each state's population was undercounted equally. That is, each state was undercounted by approximately the same amount, and "none of [the state-level ommissions rates] is statistically significantly different than the national [ommission] rate." We see that this is distinct from the results of the 2020 U.S. Census, in which the population was undercounted by 0.6%, but each state had overcount or undercount errors as large as 7% of that state's population. These errors were so large in the 2020 U.S. Census that three seats in the House of Representatives were not assigned to the correct states.

This should lead us to believe that the data collection or processing methodologies in the 2020 U.S. Census were significantly flawed. This degree of erroneous enumeration should merit significant concern, as the American democratic republic is wholly dependent on ensuring that each state can be assigned exactly the amount of representation it deserves in the House of Representatives.

Is Differential Privacy the Culprit?

An honest discussion about the errors of the 2020 United States Census must also necessarily include changes in its data collection and processing techniques since the 2010 Census. If the Census Bureau's final data were significantly more erroneous in 2020 than in 2010, then changes in collection and processing techniques should come under scrutiny as a possible cause of increased error.

What Is Differential Privacy?

In 2017, the United States Census Bureau announced that it would be implementing a new statistical method in order to protect the confidentiality of Census respondents. This new method, based on a concept called differential privacy, would take effect beginning in the 2020 U.S. Census.

The Bureau chose to implement differential privacy methods in response to concerns that increased computational power would allow malicious actors to reverse aggregation processes as performed by the Bureau in order to obtain personally identifiable information. This method is referred to as database reconstruction.

Database Reconstruction Attacks

According to the Schwarzman College's report, the United States Census Bureau conducted a database reconstruction attack on its own published data from the 2010 Census. The Census Bureau, through the reconstruction attack, reported the ability to reconstruct age, race, ethnicity, and sex information accurately for 46% of the enumerated residents (approximately 144 million people).

Evidently, based on this report from the Bureau, database reconstruction attacks represent a significant threat to the privacy of American citizens and residents. By implementing differential privacy protection algorithms in the 2020 Census, the U.S. Census Bureau hoped that the 2020 Census data would be significantly more secure.

However, when the 2020 Census data came in and the results were analyzed in the context of the Post-Enumeration Survey, it was clear that the data contained unacceptable levels of error. It is unclear whether or not new statistical methods based on differential privacy were responsible for the error; such a question can only be explored further by understanding the function of the differential privacy algorithm as well as the means by which it was employed.

A Quick Look Inside Differential Privacy

Differential privacy is a "noise infusion" technique in which randomly generated "noise" values are added to confidential individual data before aggregation. Because of this infusion, a database reconstruction attack on the published data would yield the "noisy" confidential data as opposed to the original. The amount of noise added to the dataset is tunable based on a tunable parameter ε (epsilon).

When ε = 0, an infinite amount of noise is added to the original data. In this scenario, the confidential data maintains 100% of its privacy, but the published data will retain 0% of its accuracy. When ε = ∞, no noise is added to the original data. In this scenario, the confidential data maintains 0% of its original privacy, but the published data will retain 100% of its accuracy.

The U.S. Census Bureau announced that in 2020, unlike in previous years, it would release all privacy protection information, including software source code and ε values. It appears, therefore, that this process would result in increased Census transparency and accuracy. Unfortunately, after the differential privacy techniques were announced, multiple factors have called into question the validity of the 2020 U.S. Census redistricting data.

Alabama Lawsuit against Census Bureau

In March 2021, the Census Bureau announced that U.S. redistricting data would be released in August of the same year, instead of on March 31 as originally planned. The Bureau cited Covid-19 lockdown measures limiting field operations as their primary reason for this change.

That month, the state of Alabama filed a lawsuit against the United States Census Bureau, insisting that the data be released on March 31 as originally planned. Alabama also insisted in its suit that the U.S. Census Bureau should be prohibited from using differential privacy techniques. Alabama claimed that "the privacy safeguards that the Census Bureau used for the 2010 decennial census and redistricting data appear to have worked extremely well" and that there have been "no reports that anyone outside the Bureau has gained access to the responses of a particular identified person" through a database reconstruction attack.

Alabama also noted correctly that differential privacy techniques would allow the Census Bureau to publish inaccurate values for the population of each census block, and that this in turn would affect Alabama's ability in "drawing districts that provide its residents fair representation."

Finally, Alabama insisted that the Census data be released on time (in March rather than in August) in order to allow proper time to ensure that the 2022 state elections in Alabama were conducted fairly, with properly drawn voting districts, and fully in accordance with Alabama state law.

According to the Schwarzman College's report as linked above, a total of sixteen other states joined Alabama's lawsuit. However, on September 9, 2021, the federal judges overseeing the lawsuit chose to reject both requests (see here for more information).

The Bureau's Failure to Release Privacy Techniques for Redistricting

According to the Schwarzman report, on August 12, 2021, the U.S. Census redistricting data were released as according to the updated schedule. In October, the Bureau published a 37-page report explaining differential privacy, the 2010 reconstruction attack experiment, and the allocation of noise amounts (what the value of ε should be). However, the report did not explain the process by which the Bureau decided upon the necessary values of ε.

Additionally, according to the Schwarzman report, the Census Bureau made significant changes (not specified by the report) to its differential privacy algorithm between its initial announcement and before publishing the redistricting data in August. The Census Bureau never released these changes or on how it decided upon how much noise would be added to the dataset (what the value of ε should be).

Conclusions

The 2020 United States Census contained an unacceptable degree of error in its final data. This error was distributed in an extraordinarily uneven fashion across the several states, resulting in the incorrect assignment of three Congressional seats. In comparison, the 2010 United States Census significantly undercounted the U.S. population, but did so in an equal fasion. That is, all states were undercounted to approximately the same degree.

This introduction of significant error into United States Census data aligns chronologically with the introduction of new data privacy protection techniques based on cryptographic differential privacy. A total of seventeen states joined a lawsuit against the Census Bureau to argue against the use of differential privacy techniques due to concerns that differential privacy would create an unfair redistricting process for the 2022 elections and beyond. This lawsuit was later dismissed by federal judges.

In August 2021, after the Census Bureau released redistricting data to be used beginning in 2022, it became clear that three Congressional seats had been improperly assigned due to population miscounts. According to the lawsuit, differential privacy techniques did not affect the total state population count. However, the 2020 Census had large, unexplained discrepancies in state populations with magnitudes up to 7%, unlike the 2010 Census, in which error was equally distributed among the several states.

Further research and litigation will likely soon reveal the cause for the erroneous deviations in state populations as counted by the 2020 U.S. Census, as for now it is unclear whether or not the new data privacy protection methods had a negative effect on the Census Bureau's ability to publish accurate data.
