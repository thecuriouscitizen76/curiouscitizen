Integrity in the U.S. Census

Errors and their effects in the 2020 U.S. Census
Volume I, Issue I
Published on January 23, 2023

A couple years after the 2020 Census, it became clear to the U.S. Census Bureau and to journalists that there were serious errors in its final data. Fourteen states had statistically significant miscounts of up to 7%. Three seats in the House of Representatives were assigned to states that should not have them, and consequentially three seats in the House were not assigned to states that deserve them. Have we observed a normal or expected degree of error? What factors negatively affected the integrity of the 2020 U.S. Census?

Errors in the 2020 U.S. Census

Fourteen States Miscounted in 2020 Census

In May 2022, the United States Census Bureau announced that after completing its Post-Enumeration Survey (PES), it had reason to believe that the 2020 Census significantly overcounted the United States population in eight states and significantly undercounted the United States population in six states.

The eight overcounted states were Delaware, Hawaii, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New York, Ohio, Rhode Island, and Utah. Together, these states were overcounted by 2.9%. That is, for every 100 residents of one of those states, the Census counted an additional 2.9 ghostly "residents" that did not exist.

The six undercounted states were Arkansas, Florida, Illinois, Mississippi, Tennessee, and Texas. These states were undercounted by an aggregated 2.9%. That is, for every 100 residents of one of those states, the Census failed to count 2.9 actual residents.

The 2.9% aggregate overcount and 2.9% aggregate undercount does not imply that the total error in the 2020 Census was zero, however. Because the undercounted states are, on average, significantly more populous than the overcounted states, the 2.9% aggregate undercount carries more "weight". Worked out, the 2020 U.S. Census undercounted the United States population by 0.6%. That is, the U.S. Census Bureau failed to count 6 residents for every 1000 residents of the United States.

Curious readers may investigate themselves and confirm these metrics using this article from the Census Bureau as well as these data from the 2020 U.S. Census for reference.

Effects in American Politics

The errors in the 2020 United States Census do not serve merely as an inconvenience; they significantly affected the reassignment of Congressional seats to each state. According to an analysis conducted by the Heritage Foundation, Florida should have received two additional seats in the House of Representatives. Texas should have received one additional seat. Minnesota and Rhode Island both maintained a seat that they each should have lost. Colorado gained a seat to which the state had no claim.

For an aside, let us focus on Minnesota specifically. Alarmingly, Minnesota's population was overcounted by more than 210,000 residents. However, if the U.S. Census Bureau had counted merely 26 fewer residents, the state would have lost its undeserved Congressional seat. This margin is so small that both collection and processing methodologies must be scrutinized to the greatest extent.

Errors in the 2010 U.S. Census

An honest discussion about the 2020 U.S. Census necessitates a discussion of the 2010 U.S. Census. If the 2010 Census were erroneous to the same extent as the 2020 Census, the errors are more easily attributed to systematic error and methodology. Here, we consider the results of the 2010 U.S. Census and why Americans should be concerned about the results and methodology of the 2020 U.S. Census.

At First Glance: Near-Perfect Results

At a first glance, it appears that the United States 2010 Census was flawless. The Bureau determined in 2012 that the 2010 Census had undercounted the United States population by 0.01% (~36,000 residents). This is one resident missed for every ten thousand. This appears extraordinarily accurate compared to the 0.6% undercount in the 2020 Census (which is sixty times larger than the error in 2010).

Sixteen Million Residents Missing

Unfortunately, a closer look at the 2010 Census reveals significant errors in its final data. A report published by the non-profit organization Population Reference Bureau (PRB) claims that 16 million residents were missed by the 2010 Census.

These missing residents were offset by approximately 10 million erroneous counts and 6 million imputations: residents added to the census count who were never counted directly but are assumed to exist. In total, the erroneous counts and imputations nearly perfectly offset the significant undercount of the 2010 Census.

State Populations Were Undercounted Equally

Another revealing fact noted by the PRB report linked above is that each state's population was undercounted equally. That is, each state was undercounted by approximately the same amount, and "none of [the state-level ommissions rates] is statistically significantly different than the national [ommission] rate." We see that this is distinct from the results of the 2020 U.S. Census, in which the population was undercounted by 0.6%, but each state had overcount or undercount errors as large as 7% of that state's population. These errors were so large in the 2020 U.S. Census that three seats in the House of Representatives were not assigned to the correct states.

This should lead us to believe that the data collection or processing methodologies in the 2020 U.S. Census were significantly flawed. This degree of erroneous enumeration should merit significant concern, as the American democratic republic is wholly dependent on ensuring that each state can be assigned exactly the amount of representation it deserves in the House of Representatives.

Is Differential Privacy the Culprit?

An honest discussion about the errors of the 2020 United States Census must also necessarily include changes in its data collection and processing techniques since the 2010 Census. If the Census Bureau's final data were significantly more erroneous in 2020 than in 2010, then changes in collection and processing techniques should come under scrutiny as a possible cause of increased error.

What Is Differential Privacy?

In 2017, the United States Census Bureau announced that it would be implementing a new statistical method in order to protect the confidentiality of Census respondents. This new method, based on a concept called differential privacy, would take effect beginning in the 2020 U.S. Census.

According to this report, published by the Schwarzman College of Computing at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, differential privacy methods involve integrating "carefully structured random noise" into census calculations according to a cryptographic algorithm invented in 2006. The report also claims he U.S. Census Bureau has always integrated random noise into its published data in order to protect the privacy of census respondents. However, the new approach based on differential privacy was designed to be more transparent, as in previous years, neither the error's size nor its integration mechanism was publicly released.

The Bureau chose to implement differential privacy methods in response to concerns that increased computational power would allow malicious actors to reverse aggregation processes as performed by the Bureau in order to obtain personally identifiable information. This method is referred to as database reconstruction.

Database Reconstruction Attacks

According to the Schwarzman College's report, the United States Census Bureau conducted a database reconstruction attack on its own published data from the 2010 Census. The Census Bureau, through the reconstruction attack, reported the ability to reconstruct age, race, ethnicity, and sex information accurately for 46% of the enumerated residents (approximately 144 million people).

Evidently, based on this report from the Bureau, database reconstruction attacks represent a significant threat to the privacy of American citizens and residents. By implementing differential privacy protection algorithms in the 2020 Census, the U.S. Census Bureau hoped that the 2020 Census data would be significantly more secure.

However, when the 2020 Census data came in and the results were analyzed in the context of the Post-Enumeration Survey, it was clear that the data contained unacceptable levels of error. It is unclear whether or not new statistical methods based on differential privacy were responsible for the error; such a question can only be explored further by understanding the function of the differential privacy algorithm.

Conclusions

The 2020 United States Census contained an unacceptable degree of error in its final data. This error was distributed in an extraordinarily uneven fashion across the several states, resulting in the incorrect assignment of three Congressional seats. In comparison, the 2010 United States Census significantly undercounted the U.S. population, but did so in an equal fasion. That is, all states were undercounted to approximately the same degree.
