Was Darwin Wrong?

Issues with the Theory of Evolution
Volume I, Issue I
Published on January 23, 2023

Charles Darwin's theory of evolution has been widely accepted across Western civilization for several decades. But recent work by biochemists and computer scientists is beginning to pose challenges to some of the consequences of Darwin's theories. Was Darwin wrong?

Darwin's Claims

On the Origin of Species

Let's first begin by looking at Darwin's claims. In his publication On the Origin of Species, Charles Darwin argued that new species arise through a process of natural selection: those organisms who were most capable of surviving in their natural environment were more likely to reproduce and therefore propagate their advantageous genetics. Darwin proposed that new species came into existence through a continuous process of genetic mutation and natural selection over the course of millions of years.

Implications in Genetics

It is well-established in molecular biology that nearly all life forms use exactly the same machinery. Chains of amino acids are assembled by ribosomes that read genes according to a standardized encoding that appears “virtually universal” (according to this paper) across all living organisms. These chains of amino acids then fold according to very specific combinations of intramolecular forces into proteins with a useful molecular geometry. This universality of the genetic code should indicate that all living organisms evolved from a common ancestor that used this standardized genetic encoding, and new proteins that support new species must evolve from previously existing proteins.

By an extension of Darwin's theory, new proteins must evolve through random mutation filtered by natural selection. Those genes which yield proteins which do not fold correctly, or proteins which do fold correctly but do not serve a purpose, are removed from the genetic pool by natural selection. We witness this because genes that do not produce proteins that are functional in the body place an unnecessary burden on the cell that produced those proteins; non-functional proteins have to be identified and removed, and this takes additional energy that cannot be directed to other processes. It is exactly this process of protein evolution by random mutation and natural selection which prompts a great probabilistic challenge to the theory of evolution. Though recent estimates gauge the age of the Earth to be 4.5 billion years, even the most generous estimates do not permit the evolution of the complex proteins we observe in life today in such a time.

Challenges to the Theory of Evolution

As mentioned above, one of the greatest challenges to the theory of evolution is the improbability of protein mutation. The mathematics of this situation are laid out very clearly in this article by the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. We will simply set up the problem and then go directly to the answer here for brevity. For this problem and for the remainder of this article, we will focus on human biology, but this argument should hold for any eukaryotic, sexually reproducing organism within a few orders of magnitude.

The average protein in a eukaryotic organism (this category includes all plants, animals, and humans) contains 472 amino acids (472 aa), according to this paper in the National Library of Medicine. We also assume an extremely generous mutation rate of 162 mutations per billion nucleotides per generation (from here). Further research reveals that the mutation rate in human spermatazoa (see the later section on sexual reproduction) is actually lower than this (~120 mutations per billion).

Considering our hypothetical 472 aa protein, we will compute the probability of a protein mutating 10 or more times and still folding properly. We set a minimum threshold of 10 mutations because (according to the above paper by UNC Chapel Hill) with fewer than 10 mutations the protein is likely to still fold into its original configuration.

Using the mathematics laid out by the UNC Chapel Hill paper linked above, we find using computational means that the probability of a significant mutation producing a new and functional protein is approximately one in one hundred trillion trillion trillion trillions. When written as a percentage, this probability has 47 zeros to the right of the decimal point and before any significant digits appear. Expressing the size of this number is quite difficult as nothing in the human experience is this unlikely.

Improbability of Protein Mutation

Clearly the theory of evolution requires that random mutation and natural selection identify a needle in an extraordinarily large haystack. We remind ourselves that the likelihood we computed represents the probability per generation of a new, functional gene forming in the human body. That is, this number represents the probability of a new gene forming in a human being between the time he is conceived and the time he fathers a child. The National Institute of Corrections estimates that approximately 117 billion (117,000,000,000) human beings have ever lived since 190,000 BC. This is a truly unfathomable number, but it does not even scratch the surface of one hundred trillion trillion trillion trillions (one trillion is 1,000 billions). The probability of a single mutation producing a new protein ever having occurred since 190,000 BC is therefore approximated as one in one thousand trillion trillion trillions. Again, this number is quite difficult to comprehend as its magnitude is unrivaled in the human experience.

Another Roadblock: Sexual Reproduction

Yet another challenge to the origin of new species through evolution is the process of sexual reproduction in multi-cellular organisms. Bacteria and archaebacteria are single-celled organisms; thus; any mutation to the bacteria's genetics is expected to be passed down to the bacteria's daughter cells. This is because bacteria reproduce through an asexual process called binary fission: bacteria produce duplicate genetic material and organelles and then split their cell membrane into two, yielding two daughters which are copies of the original. Because each bacterium only has one copy of its genetic material, any changes are expected to be passed to the daughters.

This process looks very different in organisms that reproduce sexually, including humans. The human body contains approximately 100 trillion cells (according to this article). Though not all human cells contain human DNA (red blood cells and hair cells do not), most cells do contain a full copy of its human's genetic code (source). However, only a fraction of human cells are sex cells: cells designed to pass genetics to the next generation (spermatazoa in males and ova in females). Therefore, in order for a mutation to be passed from parent to child, the mutation either needs to occur extremely early in fetal development or in a sex cell before fertilization. We will focus on sex cells before fertilization as there exist far more unfertilized sex cells than unborn babies.

We see that in this the aforementioned issue arises. For genetic mutations yielding new and functional proteins to be passed to the next generation, the mutation needs to occur in a sex cell. If a human were to win the "cosmic lottery" and receive a functional mutation to a bone cell in his left pinky finger, his intestinal lining, or one of his white blood cells, the mutation would not be passed to his children. Therefore, we must narrow our view even further to only mutations in human sex cells.

Males produce significantly more sex cells than females, so we will focus primarily on the likelihood of a beneficial mutation occurring in a male sex cell. Suppose that a human male wins our cosmic lottery, and one of his sperm cells undergoes a mutation creating a new generation that will produce a new, distinct, and functional protein for his future child. The average human male will produce 525 billion sperm cells during his lifetime (see here). In order for our human to transmit this new gene to his children (of which he will only have a few), he will have to get lucky once again by beating the odds of two or three in 500 billion. If these odds prove too slim, the mutated sex cell will not fertilize and the mutation will not be trasmitted to the next generation. The same argument can apply to females; however, a mutation to a human female sex cell is less likely as a mutation to a male sex cell because females produce significantly fewer sex cells during their lifetimes.

We see now that the probability of a single mutation to a gene producing a new, well-folded protein having ever occurred since 190,000 BC and having been passed to the next generation (a necessary requirement for evolution) is now one in one hundred trillion trillion trillion trillion. It should be reasonable to assume from this conclusion, therefore, that Homo sapiens has not evolved to any appreciable degree since 190,000 BC.

Intelligent Design: An Alternative Theory?

What Is Intelligent Design?

One alternative explanation for the biodiversity we observe in our world is the theory of intelligent design. The primary premise of the theory of intelligent design "holds that some features of the universe and living things are best explained by an intelligent cause rather than an undirected process such as natural selection" (see here for more information).

One prominent argument for intelligent design centers on the bacterial flagellum. The word flagellum is Latin for whip, and this describes with notable accuracy both the geometry and function of the flagellum. A flagellum is a whip-like structure on the cell membrane of some bacteria used to propel bacteria through a fluid. A flagellum is constructed of approximately 35 different proteins (see here), and if a single protein misfolds or mutates in the respective bacterial genome, the flagellum does not work. Natural selection would suggest that each of these proteins evolved one at a time. However, individually these proteins do not contribute any benefit to the bacterium, and thus the genes responsible for these proteins should be excluded from selection and eventually filtered out of the gene pool.

The theory of intelligent design proposes an alternative explanation for this conundrum: perhaps the machinery of life was carefully arranged by an intelligent designer with a comprehensive understanding of biochemistry. This eliminates the need to discuss random chance or probability, as a sufficiently intelligent designer would guide the emergence of new species correctly without a potential for error.

Distinction from Theological Arguments

The theory of intelligent design, on first inspection, appears to be a thinly veiled theological argument for the origin of life. However, the evidence for intelligent design is purely empirical, and proponents of intelligent design do not cite religious or theological arguments (e.g. the Bible's book of Genesis) to strengthen their case. It is plainly evident that the theory of intelligent design is strongly compatible with many religious explanations for the origin of life. However, intelligent design is distinct from theological explanations or theories for the origin of life in that intelligent design makes no claims about the nature or identity of the intelligent designer at the center of the theory.

Conclusions

As presented above, we conclude that Darwin's theory of evolution, as it is most commonly formulated today, is significantly challenged by evidence and analysis that is only possible using our current understanding of biochemistry and the ease of numerical calculation as enabled by today's computing. Further research in biochemistry, genetics, and computation is required to better understand the probabilistic arguments that challenge Darwinian theories to more effectively characterize our scientific explanation of the origin of life.
